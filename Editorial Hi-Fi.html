About the heroism

Nikita Lanskov·July 6, 2025

Every team has a hero — the person who stays late, who knows the one undocumented incantation that brings the cluster back, who is paged at 3am because nobody else can. We celebrate them. We should be worried about them instead.

Heroism is a symptom. It is the visible, sympathetic surface of a system that has quietly stopped being legible to everyone except one person. The hero is not the cause of fragility; they are its last line of defense — and the more reliably they save us, the longer we get to avoid asking why we keep needing to be saved.

The goal is not a team without heroes. It is a team that never needs one.

What heroism hides

When the same name appears on every postmortem as the person who resolved the incident, that is not a story about talent. It is a map of where knowledge has failed to spread. Each heroic save is a deferred investment — in documentation, in tooling, in the boring runbook nobody wanted to write.

The uncomfortable part is that heroism feels good. It feels good to be needed, and it feels good to be rescued. Both sides of that exchange have an incentive to keep it going. Breaking the loop means trading a dramatic, legible win for a diffuse, invisible one — and we are bad at valuing the invisible.

Designing it out

So the work is deliberate: write the thing down the second time you explain it. Rotate the pager. Make the heroic path slightly inconvenient and the documented path slightly easier. Reward the person who made themselves unnecessary, not the one who made themselves indispensable.

It is slower. It is less satisfying. And it is the only version that scales past the limits of a single tired human at 3am.
